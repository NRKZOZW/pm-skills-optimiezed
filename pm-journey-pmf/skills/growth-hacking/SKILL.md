# Growth Hacking

## Overview
A practical growth hacking playbook focused on the levers that matter most for achieving and strengthening product-market fit. This skill covers six growth categories — onboarding, activation, viral loops, retention, reactivation, and experiment prioritization — each with concrete experiment ideas ready to adapt and run.

## When to Use
- After initial PMF signals emerge and you want to strengthen them
- When retention or activation metrics plateau
- To design a growth experiment backlog
- When shifting from product development to growth mode
- As input to quarterly growth planning

## 1. Onboarding Optimization

Reduce time-to-value so new users experience the core benefit as quickly as possible.

### Key Principles
- **Time-to-value reduction**: Every unnecessary step between signup and the "Aha Moment" is a leak in your funnel
- **Progressive disclosure**: Show only what's needed at each step; hide advanced features until relevant
- **Personalized flows**: Tailor onboarding based on user role, goal, or segment (ask during signup)
- **Setup wizard best practices**: Pre-fill defaults, allow skipping non-essential steps, show progress indicators

### Identifying the "Aha Moment"
The Aha Moment is the specific action that correlates with long-term retention. Finding it requires:
1. Compare behaviors of retained users (Day 30+) vs churned users
2. Identify the action that most strongly predicts retention
3. Optimize onboarding to guide every user toward that action

**Examples**:
- Slack: Sending 2,000 team messages (team-level activation)
- Dropbox: Uploading one file to a synced folder
- Facebook: Adding 7 friends in 10 days
- Zoom: Hosting the first meeting

### Experiment Ideas
1. **Welcome flow A/B test**: Compare a product tour vs a task-based checklist onboarding
2. **Empty state optimization**: Replace blank screens with templates, sample data, or guided actions
3. **Onboarding email sequence**: Drip emails that reinforce key actions during the first 7 days
4. **Signup friction reduction**: Test removing optional fields, adding social login, or deferring email verification
5. **Personalized first experience**: Ask one question at signup ("What's your main goal?") and customize the dashboard accordingly

## 2. Activation Improvement

Turn signups into engaged users by ensuring they complete the key actions that predict retention.

### Framework
1. **Define activation metric**: The specific action(s) a user must complete to be considered "activated"
2. **Map the activation funnel**: List every step from signup to activation with conversion rates between each step
3. **Identify drop-off points**: Where do users abandon? Use analytics and session recordings to understand why
4. **A/B test improvements**: Focus on the highest-drop-off step first for maximum leverage

### Experiment Ideas
1. **Guided first action**: Auto-create a starter project/workspace so users see value immediately
2. **Progress indicators**: Show a setup completion bar ("You're 60% set up — complete these 2 steps")
3. **Social proof nudges**: "Teams like yours typically set up [feature] first" with a one-click action
4. **Friction audit**: Remove every field, click, or decision that is not strictly necessary for activation
5. **Activation incentive**: Offer a reward (extended trial, bonus feature) for completing activation steps

## 3. Viral and Referral Loops

Build mechanisms that turn users into acquisition channels.

### Built-In Virality
Design product features that naturally expose new potential users to your product:
- **Content sharing**: User-created content includes product branding (e.g., "Made with [Product]")
- **Collaboration invites**: Users invite teammates to collaborate within the product
- **Output sharing**: Users share results/exports that link back to the product

### Referral Programs
Structured incentives for users to bring in new users:
- **Double-sided rewards**: Both referrer and referred user get value (Dropbox model)
- **Tiered rewards**: Increasing rewards for more referrals (gamification)
- **In-product triggers**: Prompt referrals at moments of delight (after achieving a milestone)

### Network Effects
When the product becomes more valuable as more people use it:
- **Direct network effects**: More users = more value for each user (messaging, social)
- **Indirect network effects**: More users attract complementary participants (marketplace)
- **Data network effects**: More users = better data = better product for everyone (AI/ML)

### Experiment Ideas
1. **Referral program launch**: Implement a simple double-sided referral reward and measure viral coefficient
2. **Share triggers**: Add sharing prompts at moments of delight (after completing a task, hitting a milestone)
3. **Invite flow optimization**: Reduce invite friction — pre-populate email lists, one-click invite links
4. **Content watermarking**: Add tasteful product attribution to user-created exports and shared content
5. **Team expansion nudge**: For B2B products, prompt users to invite colleagues when they hit collaboration-relevant features

## 4. Retention Improvement

Keep users coming back by building habits and deepening engagement.

### Habit Loop Framework
Based on Nir Eyal's Hook Model:
1. **Trigger**: External (notification, email) or internal (boredom, anxiety, FOMO) cue that initiates the behavior
2. **Action**: The simplest behavior in anticipation of a reward (opening the app, checking a feed)
3. **Variable Reward**: Unpredictable positive outcome that creates dopamine response (new content, social validation)
4. **Investment**: User puts something into the product that makes it more valuable over time (data, connections, content, preferences)

### Retention Strategies
- **Re-engagement campaigns**: Email or push notifications triggered by inactivity (3 days, 7 days, 14 days)
- **Feature stickiness analysis**: Identify which features correlate most with weekly retention and promote them
- **Churn prediction signals**: Build models that flag at-risk users before they churn (declining login frequency, reduced feature usage)
- **Streak mechanics**: Reward consecutive usage (daily streaks, weekly goals)
- **Personalized content**: Surface relevant content or recommendations based on usage history

### Experiment Ideas
1. **Re-engagement email sequence**: Trigger personalized emails at 3, 7, and 14 days of inactivity with specific value hooks
2. **Weekly digest**: Send a summary of what happened while the user was away (new content, team activity, insights)
3. **Streak rewards**: Implement a streak counter with small rewards for consecutive daily/weekly usage
4. **Feature discovery prompts**: Surface underused features to existing users at contextually appropriate moments
5. **Churn prediction intervention**: Identify the top 3 churn signals and trigger proactive outreach when detected

## 5. Reactivation

Win back users who have churned or gone dormant.

### Strategies
- **Win-back campaigns**: Targeted outreach to churned users highlighting what's improved since they left
- **Dormant user analysis**: Understand why users became inactive — survey, interview, or analyze last actions before churn
- **"What changed" notifications**: Alert dormant users about new features, improvements, or content relevant to their past usage
- **Re-onboarding**: Treat returning users as new users — offer a fresh start with updated onboarding
- **Incentivized return**: Offer a discount, extended trial, or bonus for returning users

### Experiment Ideas
1. **Win-back email with changelog**: Send churned users a "Here's what's new" email highlighting the top 3 improvements
2. **Sunset flow survey**: When users cancel, ask one question about why and offer a targeted save (pause, downgrade, feature education)
3. **Dormant user re-onboarding**: After 30 days of inactivity, show a "Welcome back" experience highlighting new features
4. **Personal outreach**: Have customer success personally email high-value dormant accounts
5. **Return incentive test**: A/B test offering a free month vs a feature unlock for returning users

## 6. Experiment Prioritization: ICE Scoring

Use ICE scoring to prioritize your growth experiment backlog.

### Framework
For each experiment, score 1-10 on three dimensions:
- **Impact**: How much will this move the target metric if successful?
- **Confidence**: How confident are you that this will work? (Based on data, analogies, or intuition)
- **Ease**: How easy is this to implement? (Time, resources, technical complexity)

**ICE Score** = Impact x Confidence x Ease

### Scoring Guidelines
| Score | Impact | Confidence | Ease |
|-------|--------|------------|------|
| 10 | 2x+ improvement | Strong data proves it | < 1 day to ship |
| 7 | 50%+ improvement | Similar experiments worked | < 1 week |
| 5 | 20%+ improvement | Reasonable hypothesis | 1-2 weeks |
| 3 | 5-10% improvement | Educated guess | 1 month |
| 1 | Marginal improvement | Pure speculation | Multi-month project |

### Process
1. List all experiment ideas across the six growth categories
2. Score each on Impact, Confidence, and Ease (1-10)
3. Calculate ICE score and rank
4. Run the top 2-3 experiments per sprint
5. Document results and update confidence scores for future similar experiments

## Input Format
Use $ARGUMENTS to pass:
- Product name and description
- Current funnel metrics (signup → activation → retention → referral → revenue)
- Known bottlenecks or problem areas
- Growth goals and timeline
- Available resources for experimentation

## Output
A prioritized growth experiment plan including:
- Assessment of current growth bottleneck (onboarding, activation, retention, virality, or reactivation)
- 10-15 experiment ideas ranked by ICE score
- Detailed design for the top 3 experiments (hypothesis, metric, implementation plan, success criteria)
- 30-60-90 day experiment roadmap
- Measurement framework for tracking experiment results

## Tips
- Focus on one growth lever at a time — trying to improve everything simultaneously dilutes impact
- Activation and retention are almost always higher leverage than acquisition in the PMF stage
- Run experiments for statistical significance, not just until you see a result you like
- Document every experiment — wins AND losses — to build organizational learning
- The best growth hacks are product improvements, not marketing tricks
